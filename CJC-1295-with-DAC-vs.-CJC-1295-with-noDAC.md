🧬 CJC‑1295 DAC vs. CJC‑1295 No DAC — Educational Overview

Overview

CJC‑1295 is a synthetic analog of growth hormone–releasing hormone (GHRH) studied for its ability to modulate GH‑axis signaling. Two primary variants appear in research literature: CJC‑1295 with DAC (Drug Affinity Complex) and CJC‑1295 without DAC (No DAC / ND). Each form exhibits distinct pharmacokinetic and receptor‑interaction profiles, making them useful for different experimental models.

Mechanistic Differences

CJC‑1295 with DAC

The DAC modification allows the peptide to bind to albumin, significantly extending its half‑life in research models. This enables prolonged GH‑axis stimulation and sustained IGF‑1 elevation curves in controlled laboratory settings.

CJC‑1295 No DAC (ND)

The ND variant lacks the Drug Affinity Complex, resulting in a short half‑life and a GH‑pulse pattern that more closely resembles natural physiologic secretion. Research often uses this form to study acute GH‑release dynamics.

Pharmacokinetic Comparison

CJC‑1295 with DAC

Extended half‑life due to albumin binding

Sustained GH‑axis activation

Useful for long‑duration metabolic and endocrine studies

CJC‑1295 No DAC

Short half‑life (~30 minutes in many models)

Produces sharp, transient GH pulses

Useful for studying physiologic GH‑pulse timing and receptor responsiveness

Subcutaneous Administration (Research Context Only)

Both variants are commonly evaluated using subcutaneous (subq) administration models to study absorption kinetics and GH‑axis response curves. Subq routes allow researchers to examine:

Uptake rate differences between DAC and ND

Pulse amplitude vs. sustained release

Comparative receptor activation patterns

Research Themes

GH‑axis modulation and pituitary responsiveness

Metabolic and endocrine signaling pathways

Tissue‑repair and cellular‑regeneration models

Comparative pharmacokinetics between long‑acting and short‑acting GHRH analogs

Synergistic studies with GHS‑class peptides (e.g., Ipamorelin)
