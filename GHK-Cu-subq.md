🧬 GHK‑Cu (Subcutaneous) — Educational Overview
Overview

GHK‑Cu (glycyl‑L‑histidyl‑L‑lysine–copper) is a naturally occurring human tripeptide that forms a high‑affinity complex with copper(II). First identified in human plasma, GHK‑Cu has been extensively studied for its roles in tissue repair, extracellular matrix (ECM) remodeling, gene expression modulation, and cellular signaling.

The peptide exists in two primary forms:

GHK (unbound tripeptide)

GHK‑Cu (copper‑bound complex) — the biologically active form most frequently examined in research

GHK‑Cu is widely referenced in dermatologic, regenerative, and molecular biology literature for its influence on collagen dynamics, wound‑healing pathways, and cellular communication.

Physiological Context

GHK is naturally present in:

Human plasma

Saliva

Urine

Wound fluid

Skin and connective tissue matrices

Its concentration declines significantly with age, paralleling reductions in tissue repair capacity and collagen turnover.

GHK‑Cu participates in several biological processes:

Copper transport: Acts as a carrier for bioavailable copper, an essential enzymatic cofactor

ECM remodeling: Influences collagen synthesis, integrin signaling, and matrix metalloproteinase (MMP) regulation

Cellular communication: Modulates gene expression related to repair, inflammation, and oxidative stress

These properties have made GHK‑Cu a subject of ongoing research in dermatology, aging biology, and tissue‑repair models.

Receptor Signaling and Mechanistic Pathways

GHK‑Cu does not act through a single classical receptor. Instead, research describes a multi‑pathway signaling profile involving:

Copper‑dependent enzymatic activation: Supports enzymes involved in collagen cross‑linking, antioxidant defense, and angiogenesis

Gene expression modulation: Transcriptomic studies show broad up‑regulation and down‑regulation of genes related to repair, inflammation, and oxidative stress

ECM and integrin signaling: Influences fibroblast activity, keratinocyte migration, collagen I/III synthesis, and MMP/TIMP balance

Wound‑healing cascades: Impacts angiogenesis, granulation tissue formation, and remodeling phases of tissue repair

Collectively, these mechanisms position GHK‑Cu as a peptide of interest in regenerative and dermatologic research.

Scientific and Regulatory Context

Published dermatology, molecular biology, and regenerative medicine literature describes GHK‑Cu as:

A naturally occurring copper‑binding tripeptide with roles in tissue remodeling

A compound with documented effects on collagen synthesis, fibroblast activity, and gene expression

A peptide with broad transcriptomic influence, affecting pathways related to inflammation, oxidative stress, and repair

A molecule whose plasma levels decline with age, paralleling reduced regenerative capacity

Regulatory notes (U.S.):

GHK‑Cu is not FDA‑approved for any medical indication

It appears in research and compounding contexts

It is used in cosmetic formulations under cosmetic regulatory frameworks

No therapeutic claims are recognized by regulatory agencies
