🧬 Sermorelin (Subcutaneous) — Educational Overview  
Overview  

Sermorelin (sermorelin acetate) is a synthetic peptide corresponding to the first 29 amino acids of human growth hormone–releasing hormone (GHRH [1–29]NH₂). It functions as a GHRH receptor agonist, stimulating endogenous growth hormone (GH) release from the anterior pituitary rather than providing exogenous GH directly.  

Historically, sermorelin was marketed under the brand name Geref as an FDA‑approved agent for the evaluation and treatment of pediatric growth hormone deficiency. Commercial marketing was later discontinued, but the compound remains a subject of ongoing endocrine and metabolic research.   

Physiological Secretion Context  

Endogenous GHRH is produced in the hypothalamus and released into the hypophyseal portal circulation, where it acts on somatotroph cells in the anterior pituitary. Sermorelin mimics this native signaling by:  

- **Binding the GHRH receptor (GHRH‑R)** on pituitary somatotrophs  
- **Activating Gs protein–coupled signaling**, increasing intracellular cAMP  
- **Promoting calcium mobilization and GH exocytosis** from secretory granules   

GH secretion remains subject to normal physiological regulation, including:  

- Somatostatin (growth hormone–inhibiting hormone) feedback  
- Sleep architecture and circadian rhythm  
- Nutritional status and metabolic demand  
- Age‑related changes in pituitary responsiveness   

Receptor Signaling and Mechanism of Action  

Sermorelin acts at the **GHRH receptor**, a G protein–coupled receptor (GPCR) expressed on somatotroph cells:  

- **Receptor binding:** The N‑terminal residues of sermorelin (1–7) are critical for receptor activation; the C‑terminal amide (–NH₂) supports full agonist activity.   
- **Signal transduction:** GHRH‑R activation increases cAMP via adenylyl cyclase, activates protein kinase A (PKA), and triggers calcium‑dependent GH release.   
- **Pulsatile GH output:** Sermorelin enhances physiologic, pulsatile GH secretion rather than bypassing hypothalamic–pituitary regulation.  

Compared with direct GH administration, sermorelin’s mechanism preserves upstream regulatory feedback and tends to produce more physiologic GH/IGF‑1 profiles in research settings.   

IGF‑1 Axis and Downstream Effects  

As with native GH, sermorelin‑stimulated GH release leads to increased hepatic production of insulin‑like growth factor 1 (IGF‑1):  

- **IGF‑1 production:** Primarily in the liver, with additional local (paracrine/autocrine) production in peripheral tissues.  
- **Systemic actions:** IGF‑1 contributes to tissue growth, protein synthesis, and metabolic regulation.  
- **Feedback regulation:** IGF‑1 participates in negative feedback to the hypothalamic–pituitary axis, modulating further GH secretion.   

Scientific and Regulatory Context  

Published endocrine and pharmacology literature describes sermorelin as:  

- A **short, fully active GHRH fragment (1–29)NH₂** that retains full agonist activity at GHRH‑R.   
- A compound with documented use in **diagnostic testing** of GH reserve and **therapeutic trials** in pediatric GH deficiency.   
- A peptide with a **short plasma half‑life (~10–20 minutes)**, motivating later development of longer‑acting GHRH analogs.   

Regulatory history (U.S.):  

- Initial FDA approval for diagnostic use (1990) and therapeutic use in pediatric GH deficiency (1997).   
- Voluntary discontinuation from the U.S. market for commercial reasons; subsequent FDA notice confirmed withdrawal was not based on safety or efficacy concerns.   
- Ongoing reference in research and compounding contexts, including listing as a Category 1 bulk substance under the FDA 503A framework.
